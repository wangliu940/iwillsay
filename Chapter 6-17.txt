The president's remarks were in the midst of his first news conference since losing the campaign, and were not expected to trigger a congressional vote on the debt limit.

But Trump's administration has signaled it is willing to revisit its position on the debt limit.

"We've got to do it. We're going to do it," Trump told reporters.

A major element of the debt ceiling debate was whether the Bush administration should have been able to keep it up. Then-Sen. John McCain, R-Ariz., said the president's position was different from that of previous presidents, who had to rely on the debt limit.

"When the debt ceiling is raised, the president can't continue to keep it up," McCain said.

The administration has said it will continue to pay its bills — to the tune of $1.2 trillion — if Congress does not agree to the deal.

It has also said it will not raise the debt ceiling until the Senate passes a budget resolution.

Senate Majority Leader Mitch McConnell, R-Ky., has said the Senate would have to vote on a spending bill during a break in negotiations with the White House.
